Shing-Chi Cheung (Hong Kong University of Science and Technology, China)

Title: GreenDroid: Diagnosis of Energy Inefficiency for Android Applications

Abstract: Smartphone applications’ energy efficiency is vital, but many Android applications suffer from serious energy inefficiency problems. Locating these problems is labor-intensive and automated diagnosis is highly desired. However, a key challenge is the lack of a decidable criterion that facilitates automated judgment of such energy problems. We have conducted a study of 173 open-source and 229 commercial Android applications to examine this challenge. Our findings suggests that many of energy complaints by users arose from ineffective usage of sensors. In this talk, we review the energy inefficiency problems in Android applications and explore how they may be diagnosed. We introduce an approach that can automatically analyze an application’s sensory data utilization in different states, and locate ineffective sensor usage scenarios. We realized the approach as a tool, GreenDroid, on top of Java PathFinder, and successfully found real energy problems in popular Android applications. In addition, GreenDroid found new unreported energy problems that were later confirmed by developers.

Shi Han (Microsoft Research Asia, China)

Title: Context-Sensitive OS Performance Analysis

Abstract: Operating system (OS) performance is a fundamental factor to the usage experience of its applications, and then is critical to the popularity and success of its corresponding software ecosystem. OS performance analysis is highly challenging given large-scale code bases and highly complex runtime situations. The top challenges include the identification of performance bottlenecks with non-deterministic reproduction of performance degradations. And the underlying insight is that a program site may trigger performance degradations and be considered as a performance bottleneck only under some specific call contexts, especially across multiple components from different layers. Therefore, context-sensitive analysis would be required for identifying performance bottlenecks in large-scale and highly complex OS software products.

In this talk, the presenter will introduce two research projects to exemplify context-sensitive techniques in OS performance analysis. The two research projects are selected from a comprehensive framework for OS performance analysis, covering both development-site and deployment-site, benefiting both proactive prediction and postmortem analysis, helping both feature-owner developers/testers and centralized analysts. The research work at the Software Analytics group of Microsoft Research Asia is based on their long-term collaborations with Microsoft product teams and academia.

Bio: Shi Han is a researcher in the Software Analytics group at Microsoft Research Asia. His research interests include data-driven software analysis, machine learning, and large-scale computing platform. Incorporating expertise from these domains, he has been pursuing research on performance analysis for large-scale system software. Since 2009, he has been the key contributor to StackMine - a scalable stack-trace mining platform for Windows performance debugging in the large. Prior to 2009, he was a key contributor to the research and development of the HMM-based East Asian language handwriting recognition engine in Windows 7. Zhenjiang Hu (National Institute of Informatics, Japan)

Title: Stepping Back to Leap Forward: Putback-based Approach to Dependable Bidirectional Programming

Abstract: A bidirectional transformation consists of pairs of transformations -- a forward transformation get produces a target view from a source, while a backward transformation put puts back modifications on the view to the source --- satisfying sensible roundtrip properties. Existing bidirectional approaches are get-based in that one writes a forward transformation and a corresponding backward transformation can be automatically derived. However, the unavoidable ambiguity of put often leads to unpredictable bidirectional behavior, making it hard to solve nontrivial practical synchronization problems. Theoretically, this ambiguity problem could be solved by writing put directly and deriving get, but how to make such an approach work in practice remains an open problem. In this paper, we propose a novel linguistic framework for supporting a putback-based approach to bidirectional programming: a new language is designed for specifying primitive putback transformations, an automatic algorithm is given to statically check necessary conditions for a put to be valid, and a derivation algorithm is provided to construct an efficient get from a valid put. A particularly elegant and strong aspect of our design is that we can simply reuse and apply standard results for tree transducers in the specification of our algorithms.

Bio: Zhenjiang Hu is a full professor of National Institute of Informatics (NII) in Japan. He received his BS and MS from Shanghai Jiao Tong University in 1988 and 1991, respectively, and PhD degree from University of Tokyo in 1996. He was a lecturer (1997–1999) and an associate professor (2000–2007) in University of Tokyo, before joining NII as a full professor in 2008. His main interest is in programming languages and software engineering in general, and functional programming, parallel programming, and bidirectional model-driven software development in particular. He is the academic committee chair of the NII Shonan Meetings, an IFIP WG 2.1 member, and is serving on the steering committees of ICFP, Haskell, APLAS, ICMT, and BX. He also belongs to the editorial boards of IEEE Transactions of Software Engineering, Science of Computer Programming, and Software and Systems Modeling.

Gang Huang (Peking University, China)

Title: Runtime Software Architecture Based Cloud Management

Abstract: System management becomes increasingly complex and brings high costs, especially with the advent of Cloud Computing. In a Cloud, numerous platforms like Virtual Machines (VMs) and Middleware have to be managed to make the whole system work cost-effectively after an application is deployed. For controlling the management cost, in particular the manual management cost, many computer programs have been developed to take over manual management tasks or reduce their complexity and difficulty. These programs are usually hard-coded by languages like Java and C++, which bring enough capability and flexibility but also cause high programming effort and cost. In this talk, I will introduce our architecture based approach to developing the management programs in a simple but powerful enough manner. The experiment on a real cloud and the comparison with the programming language approach demonstrate the feasibility, effectiveness and benefits of the new approach for management program development.

Zhong Shao (Yale University, USA) 

Title: CertiKOS: Compositional Layered Specification and Verification of a Hypervisor OS Kernel

Abstract: Operating System (OS) kernels and hypervisors form the backbone of all system software. They can have the greatest impact on the resilience, extensibility, and security of today's computing hosts. Recent effort on seL4 has demonstrated the feasibility of building large scale formal proofs of functional correctness for a general-purpose microkernel, but the cost of such verification is still prohibitive, and it is unclear how to use such a verified kernel to reason about user-level programs and other kernel extensions.

In this talk, I will present a novel compositional approach for formally specifying and verifying OS kernels. Because the very purpose of an OS kernel is to build layers of abstractions over hardware resources, we insist on uncovering and specifying these layers formally and then performing the verification of each kernel function at its proper abstraction layer. To support linking with other kernel extensions and user-level programs, we prove a stronger contextual refinement property for every kernel function, which states that the implementation of each such function will behave like its functional specification under any kernel/user or host/guest context. Our abstraction layers are defined as assembly-level machines extended with abstract kernel primitives, but almost all of our kernel programs are written in a variant of CompCert Clight language, verified at the source level, and compiled and linked together using a modified version of the CompCert compiler. To demonstrate the effectivness of our new methodology, we have successfully implemented and specified a realistic hypervisor OS kernel and verified its (contextual) functional correctness property in the Coq proof assistant. Our hypervisor kernel is written in 3000 lines of C and x86 assembly, and can successfully boot a version of Linux as a guest. The entire specification and proof effort took less than one person year.
Bio:  Zhong Shao is Professor of Computer Science at Yale University.  He received his PhD degree in Computer Science from Princeton University in 1994.  His research interests include certified code,  programming languages and compilers, program verification, formal  methods, and operating systems.  He was a key developer of the Standard ML of New Jersey system and the architect of the FLINT certifying compiler infrastructure. He currently leads the Yale FLINT  group to build a practical infrastructure for constructing large-scale certified system software.

Yingfei Xiong (Peking University, China)

Title: Automatic Memory Leak Fixing for C Programs

Abstract: This talk reports our attempt towards automatically fixing memory leaks in C programs. In our approach, we turn the problem of memory-leak fixing into the problem of generating fixes for as many as possible leaky paths, ensuring the correctness of the fixes. In particular, we abstract control flow graphs (CFGs) into directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) by collapsing loops, and build procedure summaries to aid inter-procedure analysis. The statement inside loops are handled separately. By using DAGs we can ensure correctly and efficiently generating fixes for memory leaks via traversing DAGs.

We implemented our inter-procedural memory leak fixing into a tool named LeakFix and evaluated LeakFix on 14 programs with 495k lines of code. Our evaluation shows that LeakFix is able to successfully fix a substantial number of memory leaks. Furthermore, both the execution time and memory consumption of LeakFix are low, indicating that LeakFix is scalable for large applications.

Bio: Yingfei Xiong is an assistant professor under the young talents plan at Peking University. He got his Ph.D. degree from the University of Tokyo in 2009 and worked as a postdoctoral fellow at University of Waterloo between 2009 and 2011. His research interest is consistency management.


Lu Zhang (Peking University, China)

Title: Is This a Bug or an Obsolete Test?

Abstract: In software evolution, developers typically need to identify whether the failure of a test is due to a bug in the source code under test or the obsoleteness of the test code when they execute a test suite. We view this problem as a classification problem and propose an automatic approach based on machine learning. Specifically, we target Java software using the JUnit testing framework and collect a set of features that may be related to failures of tests. Using this set of features, we adopt the Best-first Decision Tree Learning algorithm to train a classifier with some existing regression test failures as training instances. Then, we use the classifier to classify future failed tests. Our preliminary evaluation demonstrates that our approach may effectively classify the causes of failed tests.

Bio: Lu Zhang is a professor at the School of Electronics Engineering and Computer Science, Peking University, P.R. China. He received both PhD and BSc in Computer Science from Peking University in 2000 and 1995 respectively. He was a postdoctoral researcher in Oxford Brookes University and University of Liverpool, UK. He has been on the editorial board of Journal of Software Maintenance and Evolution: Research and Practice and is now on the editorial board of Software Testing, Verification and Reliability. His current research interests include software testing and analysis, program comprehension, software maintenance and evolution, software reuse, and service computing.

Xiangyu Zhang (Purdue University, USA) 

Title: Improving Uncertain Data Processing by Program Analysis

Abstract: Uncertainty in data is ubiquitous, arising from multiple sources including application semantics, measurement errors, calibration errors, limited sampling, and insufficient representation precision. Properly handling such uncertainty is an important requirement for data processing, especially in the era of "Big Data", in which a lot of critical decisions are made based on uncertain data processing. In this talk, I will present our recent work in using program analysis to improve uncertain data processing, including (1) using a lightweight dynamic analysis to monitor execution so that the number of Monte Carlo samples needed to understand the effect of uncertainty can be reduced; (2) and a prediction technique that can detect if an execution is stable in the presence of floating point precision errors.

Bio: Xiangyu Zhang is a University Faculty Scholar and Associate Professor at Purdue University. His research interests include program analysis and security. He has won the ACM SIGPLAN Distinguished Dissertation Award, NSF Career Award, and ACM SIGSOFT Distinguished Paper Award.
